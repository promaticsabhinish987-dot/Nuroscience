# Human Nature Lab — The Demand Stack

## Why does this exist?

If you want to predict what people will buy, watch, follow, share, or spend money on, you must understand:

> Humans do not buy products.
>
> Humans buy solutions to unmet needs.

A product is just a vehicle.

People don't buy a car.

They buy:

* Transportation
* Safety
* Status
* Identity

depending on their situation.

---

# The Core Principle

Every human has multiple needs.

But needs are not equally active.

The strongest unmet need dominates behavior.

Think of it like a CPU scheduler.

```text
Many needs exist
↓
One becomes dominant
↓
Behavior follows
```

A starving person and a billionaire can see the same product and value it completely differently.

---

# Human Demand Hierarchy

A practical version for business:

```text
Level 1: Survival
Level 2: Security
Level 3: Comfort
Level 4: Belonging
Level 5: Status
Level 6: Achievement
Level 7: Identity
Level 8: Legacy
```

---

# Level 1 — Survival

## What humans want

Stay alive.

Needs:

* Food
* Water
* Shelter
* Basic healthcare

Questions:

* Can I eat?
* Can I survive tomorrow?

Examples:

* Rice
* Wheat
* Cheap housing
* Basic medicine

---

## Buying Behavior

Decision rule:

```text
Lowest cost
Highest necessity
```

Brand matters little.

Status matters almost zero.

---

## Example

A person earning ₹8,000/month.

Thinking:

```text
Can I pay rent?
Can I buy food?
Can I survive?
```

Not:

```text
Which luxury watch should I buy?
```

---

# Level 2 — Security

Survival is solved.

Now uncertainty becomes painful.

Needs:

* Stable income
* Insurance
* Savings
* Job security

Questions:

```text
What if something goes wrong?
```

Examples:

* Fixed deposits
* Insurance
* Emergency funds

---

## Buying Behavior

People pay to reduce risk.

This is why insurance companies exist.

They sell:

```text
Peace of mind
```

not policies.

---

# Level 3 — Comfort

Now survival and security are handled.

People optimize convenience.

Needs:

* Better house
* Better transportation
* Better lifestyle

Examples:

* Air conditioners
* Washing machines
* Food delivery

Question:

```text
How can life become easier?
```

---

## Buying Behavior

Time becomes valuable.

People start paying for convenience.

# Level 4 — Belonging

Needs:

* Family

* Acceptance

Questions:


Examples:

Because belonging matters.

# Level 5 — Status

# But There Is a Trap

Many people buy from higher levels before lower levels are solved.

Examples:

```text
Poor person buys luxury phone.
```

Why?

Because status competes with survival.

Humans are emotional, not purely rational.

# Real Demand Formula

Demand is roughly:

```text
Demand =
Need Intensity
×
Ability To Pay
×
Awareness
```

A person may desperately need something.

But if they cannot pay:

```text
Demand → No transaction
```

A person may have money.

But no awareness:

```text
Demand → No transaction
```

All three matter.

---

Think of every person as holding four currencies.

```text
Money
Time
Attention
Social Capital
```

Every purchase is a trade.

Examples:

Netflix

```text
Money → Entertainment
```

Instagram

```text
Attention → Entertainment
```

University

```text
Money + Time
→ Future opportunities
```

Luxury watch

```text
Money
→ Status signal
```

# Core Signal

People do not buy products.

They trade resources (money, time, attention) to satisfy the strongest active need in their current life situation.

# Highest Leverage Insight

The biggest business mistake is selling a Level 5 solution (status) to a Level 1 customer (survival).

Match the offer to the dominant unmet need.

# System Model

```text
Resources
    ↓
Active Need
    ↓
Perceived Value
    ↓
Decision
    ↓
Purchase
```

# 3-Line Summary

Humans have multiple needs, but the strongest unmet need drives behavior.

As resources increase, demand shifts from survival toward status, identity, achievement, and legacy.

The best businesses identify the dominant need of a specific customer segment and build for that need.

# 1-Line Summary

People buy whatever best satisfies their highest-priority unmet need within their available resources.

# The Stronger Model

Instead of:

```text
Money
↓
Psychology
↓
Behavior
```

Use:

```text
Resources
↓
Active Need
↓
Psychology
↓
Behavior
↓
Purchase
```

Because what matters most is not how much money someone has.

What matters is:

> Which need is currently strongest and most painful.

A startup founder with ₹5 crore may still buy achievement products. A celebrity with ₹100 crore may still buy status products. A retiree with ₹50 crore may primarily buy legacy products.

The active need drives the behavior; money determines how many options are available to satisfy it.
