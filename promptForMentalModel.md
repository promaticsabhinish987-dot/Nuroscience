## Redesign Protocol

If you answer **Yes** to questions 1, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8, or 9 — or cannot answer 2 or 10 clearly:

| Failure question | Redesign move |
|----------------|----------------|
| 1 (Wrapper trap) | Strip to physical goods. Ask: what breaks in real world? |
| 2 (Phantom pain) | Interview 5 users. Ask "what's the worst feeling here?" not "what do you need?" |
| 3 (Utility graveyard) | Add a Path B layer: status, ritual, identity, belonging, aesthetic |
| 4 (Identity void) | Make usage visible or sharable. Add a symbol, badge, or social signal |
| 5 (Zero switching cost) | Build stored value (data, content, relationships, history) user cannot export easily |
| 6 (Invisible tool) | Add a social component — even just a simple "used by X" or shareable result |
| 7 (Habit fight) | Redesign to fit existing 3-minute window in user's current day |
| 8 (Paradox overload) | Reduce to 3 choices max. Hide advanced options behind "more" button |
| 9 (Forgotten ending) | Add a micro-closing ritual: sound, animation, confirmation, gratitude |
| 10 (Incomplete sentence) | Go back to step 2. You don't understand the emotion yet |

## The 10 Failure Questions

For each, answer honestly. One "yes" is a warning. Two or more = stop and redesign.

---

### 1. The Wrapper Trap
> *"If all software and finance disappeared tonight, would anyone still care about this problem by tomorrow morning?"*

- **If No:** You're building a derivative. The real problem is elsewhere. Redesign by finding the physical good underneath.
- **Failure mode:** Solving digital friction while physical supply chain breaks.

---

### 2. The Phantom Pain
> *"Is the user's stated problem actually a symptom — and have you named the real emotion hiding underneath?"*

- **If you can't name the emotion (fear, shame, loneliness, boredom, disgust, confusion):** You'll build features that miss the real need.
- **Failure mode:** Solving "I need X" when they really need "I need to stop feeling Y."

---

### 3. The Utility Graveyard
> *"Does your product live entirely in Path A (utility & control) with no Path B layer (identity & flow)?"*

- **If Yes:** You are building a commodity. Someone will undercut you on price or speed. You will die slowly.
- **Failure mode:** Winning on efficiency → becoming invisible → being replaced.

---

### 4. The Identity Void
> *"Would the user feel embarrassed or neutral — not proud — to tell a friend they use your product?"*

- **If Yes:** Zero identity signal. No emotional stickiness. You are a tool, not a tribe.
- **Failure mode:** Utility without status = disposable.

---

### 5. The Zero Switching Cost
> *"If a slightly better or cheaper alternative appeared tomorrow, would the user feel any emotional loss leaving you?"*

- **If No:** You have no moat. Your "loyalty" is just lack of options.
- **Failure mode:** Product as rental, not relationship.

---

### 6. The Invisible Tool
> *"Can someone use your product for a year without anyone else noticing?"*

- **If Yes:** No social proof, no network effect, no visible identity signal. You are a utility in the dark.
- **Failure mode:** Private value only. Social insects win. Lone wolves die.

---

### 7. The Habit Fight
> *"Does using your product require the user to change an existing behavior — rather than slide into an existing rhythm?"*

- **If Yes:** You are fighting human nature. Most will try once, then revert.
- **Failure mode:** Perfect solution to a problem no one will change their life to reach.

---

### 8. The Paradox Overload
> *"Does your product give the user more than 3 meaningful choices at any critical decision point?"*

- **If Yes:** You are causing anxiety, not reducing it. Choice = pain above a low threshold.
- **Failure mode:** More features = less satisfaction. Nokia's ghost.

---

### 9. The Forgotten Ending
> *"What is the last thing the user feels before they stop using your product each time?"*

- **If you don't know, or if it's anything other than a small positive:** Your peak-end rule is broken. They will remember frustration, not value.
- **Failure mode:** Great middle, terrible exit = negative memory = no return.

---

### 10. The Incomplete Sentence
> *"Can you complete this sentence without lying?"*
> *"This product will win because it reduces the user's feeling of ______ while increasing their feeling of ______ — without requiring them to ______."*

- **If you cannot fill all three blanks honestly and specifically:** Your idea is not ready. Go back to step 2 or 3.
- **Failure mode:** Fuzzy value proposition = fuzzy retention = fuzzy death.

---

## The One-Minute Audit

If you have 60 seconds:

> *1. Physical foundation? 2. Hidden emotion? 3. Path A or B? 4. Any parameter below 4? 5. Can I complete the sentence?*

Fail any → redesign.

Now you have a **failure detector**, not just a thinking tool.













```
You are about to evaluate an idea, product, or market.

Before you accept any consensus answer, run it through this sequence:

1. Strip the wrapper
What is the physical goods foundation underneath this? (Layer 1–5 from the umbrella — food, shelter, energy, movement, raw materials.)
If software or finance vanished tomorrow, would the problem still exist?
If yes — that’s the real problem. If no — you’re working on a derivative.

2. Find the unstated psychological need
The stated problem is never the real problem.
Ask: What feeling is the user actually trying to achieve?
Not “save time” — but “feel less anxious.”
Not “find information” — but “feel less lost.”
Name the emotion hiding under the feature request.

3. Identify which path you’re on
Path A (Utility & Control): Solves tasks, measurable efficiency, substitutable.
Path B (Identity & Flow): Changes how user feels about themselves, hard to leave.
If Path A — ask: Can I add a Path B layer without breaking utility?
If Path B — ask: Is the utility good enough to earn initial trust?

4. Score against the 7 psychological parameters
Rate 1–10 for each. Any parameter below 4 is a potential fatal flaw.

Parameter	Score (1–10)
Cognitive load (lower = better)	
Identity signal (higher = better for long-term)	
Emotional switching cost (higher = better)	
Social proof visibility (higher = better)	
Default behavior fit (higher = better)	
Avoids paradox of choice (higher = better)	
Peak-end positive memory (higher = better)	
5. Apply the inversion test
Take the consensus advice about this idea (e.g., “launch fast,” “add more features,” “avoid competition”).
Find one counterexample where that advice failed.
Extract the hidden variable the advice ignored.
Ask: What would the opposite of consensus advice look like here?

6. Final filter — the one sentence
Complete this sentence for your idea:
“This product will win because it reduces the user’s feeling of ______ while increasing their feeling of ______ — without requiring them to ______.”
If you cannot complete it honestly, the idea is not ready.  --> break 10 repeated qustions that leads to failure and redesign it
```
