You’re pointing at something real, but it’s not quite as simple as “the world is decided entirely by internal state.” The external world still exists and constrains us—but our experience of it is heavily filtered. A more complete “architecture” looks like layers interacting, not a single cause.

Here’s a clearer model:

---

### 1. **Raw Reality (External Input)**

This is the physical world—light, sound, events, other people. It’s the same baseline environment for everyone (more or less).

But we never experience this directly.

---

### 2. **Biological State (Body & Brain)**

Your body shapes perception before thought even begins.

* Health vs illness (e.g., fever, fatigue)
* Hormones, neurotransmitters
* Nervous system sensitivity

Someone with Depression literally processes stimuli differently—neutral events can feel negative. Anxious systems scan for threats; relaxed systems don’t.

---

### 3. **Perception Filters (Sensory + Attention)**

Your brain selects what to notice.

* Attention (what you focus on)
* Pattern recognition
* Threat vs reward detection

Two people in the same room will “see” different things because their brains prioritize differently.

---

### 4. **Interpretation Layer (Meaning-Making)**

This is where identity and beliefs start shaping reality.

* Beliefs (“people are trustworthy” vs “people are dangerous”)
* Past experiences
* Cultural conditioning

The same event—someone laughing—can be interpreted as:

* “They like me”
* “They’re mocking me”

---

### 5. **Identity / Role System**

This is what you were getting at with “joker” or “hero.”

Identity acts like a lens that organizes interpretation:

* “I’m a victim” → sees injustice everywhere
* “I’m a hero” → sees challenges to overcome
* “I’m a clown/joker” → sees absurdity and humor

This connects loosely to ideas popularized by Carl Jung (archetypes) and modern psychology’s “self-schema.”

Identity doesn’t just interpret reality—it *predicts* it.

---

### 6. **Emotional State**

Emotions are not just reactions—they shape perception in real time.

* Fear narrows focus
* Joy broadens it
* Anger biases toward blame

This creates a feedback loop:
**state → perception → interpretation → stronger state**

---

### 7. **Narrative / Story Layer**

Humans constantly build stories:

* “This always happens to me”
* “I’m the kind of person who…”
* “The world is…”

This narrative stabilizes identity over time, even if it’s inaccurate.

---

### 8. **Behavior & Feedback Loop**

What you do affects what happens next.

* If you expect hostility → act defensive → others respond negatively → confirms belief
* If you expect opportunity → act open → get different responses

This is where subjective reality starts shaping *objective outcomes*.

---

## Putting It Together

A simplified flow:

**External world → Body state → Attention → Interpretation → Identity → Emotion → Narrative → Behavior → Feedback → (loops back)**

---

## Key Clarification

* You’re right that *internal state + identity strongly shape experienced reality.*
* But it’s not total control—reality pushes back.
* It’s better described as a **controlled hallucination constrained by sensory input** (a concept linked to neuroscience ideas like predictive processing).

---

## A Practical Insight

If someone wants to “change their world,” the most leverage points are:

* Regulating body (sleep, health)
* Shifting attention (what you notice)
* Questioning identity (“is this the only role I can play?”)
* Updating narratives

Trying to change the world without changing these layers is much harder.
