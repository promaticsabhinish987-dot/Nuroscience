## Your core claim (restated)

> The software industry only provides a *platform* for real problems to be displayed and solved.  
> The *real* problems — the deep, primary ones — are **real goods related** (physical, tangible, material).

Software is a layer.  
The foundation is atoms, not bits.

---

## 1. Why this distinction matters

Most startup thinking treats software as *the* product.  
But in truth:

- Software **optimizes**, **connects**, **measures**, **automates** — it rarely creates the fundamental value from scratch.
- The fundamental value comes from:
  - Food
  - Shelter
  - Clothing
  - Energy
  - Transportation (physical movement)
  - Healthcare (physical bodies)
  - Raw materials
  - Manufactured goods

Software sits *around* these, not *under* them.

Example:  
Uber’s software is the platform.  
The real problem is: “A human needs to move physically from A to B using a real car, real fuel, real tires, real roads.”  
Software just coordinates the existing real goods.

---

## 2. The complete umbrella image

Here’s the hierarchy from **most fundamental** to **least fundamental**:

| Layer | What it is | Example |
|-------|------------|---------|
| **1. Physical survival goods** | Things your body needs to exist | Air, water, food, shelter, clothing, medicine |
| **2. Physical production goods** | Things used to make other physical things | Machinery, tools, raw materials (steel, wood, lithium) |
| **3. Physical movement goods** | Things that move people or other physical goods | Vehicles, ships, pipelines, roads, trains |
| **4. Physical energy goods** | Things that power the above | Oil, gas, electricity, batteries, nuclear fuel |
| **5. Real goods transformation** | Turning raw physical goods into finished physical goods | Factories, refineries, assembly lines |
| **6. Software layer** | Instructions running on silicon, coordinating the above | Apps, platforms, databases, algorithms |
| **7. Financial layer** | Claims on future value, sitting on top of everything | Money, stocks, bonds, derivatives |

**Your insight is correct:**  
Layers 6 and 7 (software and finance) are *wrappers* around layers 1–5.  
When software solves a problem *without* touching layers 1–5, it’s often solving a secondary problem — or a derivative problem.

---

## 3. What this means for “real problems”

A **real problem** in the deepest sense means:

> A failure in the availability, quality, cost, or distribution of **layers 1–5**.

Software can *reveal* that failure (via data, dashboards, alerts).  
Software can *partially address* that failure (via matching supply to demand, optimizing routes, automating inventory).  
But software cannot *replace* the physical good itself.

Examples:

| Problem | Is the root physical? | Software’s role |
|---------|----------------------|------------------|
| People are hungry | Yes (lack of food) | Coordinates delivery, predicts demand, reduces waste |
| Hospital has no beds | Yes (physical beds, physical space) | Manages bed allocation, tracks occupancy |
| Factory runs out of steel | Yes (raw material shortage) | Forecasts inventory, automates reordering |
| City has no clean water | Yes (physical pipes, treatment plants) | Monitors flow, detects leaks, bills customers |

If the physical good vanishes, the software becomes worthless instantly.

---

## 4. The trap most founders fall into

They build software that solves a problem **entirely inside layer 6**:

- Better to-do list app
- Another calendar tool
- AI writing assistant
- Social media scheduler

These are solving *digital-native* problems.  
They are real *within the digital world*, but they are **derivative** of the physical world.

The deeper, more durable, more fundamental problems are always:

> “How do we get more physical goods of higher quality to more people using less energy and less waste?”

Software helps answer that. But the problem itself is not software.

---

## 5. The complete picture you asked for

Imagine an **upside-down umbrella**:

- **The handle (touching the ground)** = Physical goods (layers 1–5)  
- **The canopy (spreading above)** = Software + finance + services  

Most people look at the canopy and think that’s the whole thing.  
You’re correctly pointing to the handle — the thing that actually touches reality.

So when you ask “how is an idea generated?” —  
If you want a *foundational* idea (not a derivative one), start with:

> A failure in the **physical chain** from raw material → finished good → human need.

Then ask:  
> Where can software *wrap around* this physical failure to fix it faster or cheaper?

That’s the umbrella.  
The handle is physics.  
The canopy is code.  
Most startups build pretty canopies attached to nothing.  
The real ones attach to a solid handle.
